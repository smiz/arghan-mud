Characters

Basic attributes, levels, and experience points

The character system is based on 5th Edition D&D. You have six attributes: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma. The values of your attributes are determined at random by a roll of 3d6. The primary effect of your attributes is to modify skill attempts and saving throws. Constitution modifies your hit points, supplying a bonus or penalty to each hit die roll. Strength effects your chance to hit and do damage with heavy weapons, like swords, spears, and axes. Dexterity has this effect for light weapons, like daggers and knives. The modifiers accorded to each attribute score are listed in the table below.

You character has a level, which begins at zero. Initially and with each subsequent level, you gain 1d6 hit points plus your Constitution modifier (but always at least 1). With each level gained, the skills you already possess each improve by a point. You also gain a practice slot that can be expended to increase an existing skill or learn a new skill. To advance from level zero to level one requires 10 experience points. Each subsequent level requires your current level times 20 to advance. For example, to advance from level 3 to 4 requires 60 experience points.

How do you gain experience points? In two ways. Successfully scoring damage in combat gains experience equal to the number of points of damage you score. The majority of your experience points will likely come from gathering items. Treasure items have an experience point value that is claimed by the first person to pickup the item. The experience point value of the item is commensurate with the difficulty of acquiring it.

Can you lose levels and experience? Absolutely! If your character is killed, you automatically lose one level of experience. Your experience needed to advance is reset as if you had just attained that level. With this loss of level, you lose the hit points, skill points, and practice slots earned when the leveling up.

Skill checks and saving throws

A skill check involve rolling 1d20 plus the skill points plus an attribute modifier and comparing this to a target for success. A saving throw is identical expect that it may or may not involve a skill. Saving throws happen automatically, usually in response to traps or special effects. Skill checks happen whenever you attempt to use the skill or enter into a situation that requires the skill to process; e.g., the climb a wall or swim across a body of water. The specific skills available in the MUD are described in the sections below.

Climbing

This skill allows you to traverse sheer surfaces. The target is set by the surface you are attempting to climb. The skill modifier is determined by the average of your Strength and Dexterity.

Literacy

Your ability to read. The modifier for this skill is Intelligence. The target is determined by the difficulty of the text you are trying to comprehend. This skill complements the magic skill. You must read scrolls and books to learn spells. The magic skill is used to cast them.

Magic

This is your skill at shaping the word with words of power. The difficulty of a skill check is determined by the intensity of the effect you desire to achieve. Specifically, the target is 10 + 2 * intensity. Success results in the effect; failure causes a loss of hit points as uncontrolled magic tears through your body.

Melee

This is the skill of hand to hand combat. Your target to succeed is the armor class of your opponent. Success means you hit the target. The skill modifier is Strength for heavy weapons, like swords and axes. The modifier is Dexterity for light weapons, like knives and daggers.

Perception

This is your ability to perceive subtle actions and events. This skill is used to spot sneaking monsters, to sense when someone is trying to steal from or swindle you, and anytime you face subtlety and subterfuge. The modifying attribute is Wisdom.

Steal

You have light fingers. This skill is used to take an item from the target's inventory. The steal check is opposed to the target's perception check. The modifying attribute is Dexterity.

Stealth

You can move unseen and unheard. The modifier is your Dexterity. A stealth check is pitted against a Perception check of the monster who may detect your presence. A sneaking monster remains hidden until they take some action that gives them away, such as attacking or speaking. If you attack while sneaking, then you have the advantage of surprise.

Swimming

This skill allows you to move through water. The target will be set by the body of water that you are attempting to cross. The skill modifier is determined by the average of your Strength and Constitution.

Swindle

You have a forked tongue and you use it to part fools from their treasure. If you successfully swindle an monster, you can acquire an item from their person. Your swindle check is opposed to the marks perception check. The modifying attribute is Charisma.
